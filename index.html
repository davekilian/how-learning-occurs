How Learning Occurs

A journey deep into the center of the human brain, by Dave Kilian.

Made for EDMA 5043: Learning Psychology and Student Needs,
as part of Northwest University's Master in Teaching program.

Right now, somewhere in the world ...

... someone is learning  to count. to talk. to share their toys. arithmetic. to write a persuasive essay. about the structure of DNA. to solve equations. world history. to change their car's oil. to bake a cake.

Learning, rather than discovering — because someone else is teaching them!

The ability to pass down knowledge is one of humanity's superpowers. It's the cornerstone of our technological progress: nobody needs to reinvent the wheel, because we inherited the invention of wheel from our ancestors. By passing down our greatest discoveries and our best tools, we give each new generation the best possible starting point to make discoveries and tools of their own. It's an endless cycle of technological improvement!

If I have seen further, it is by standing on the shoulders of giants.

—Sir Isaac Newton, 1675

But how does this uniquely human ability work? The answer is of particular interest to any teacher: after all, our job is to facilitate our students' learning! To answer this question, we'll have to learn a few things about the inner workings of the human brain.

The first thing to know: inside, brains are complex and messy.

This shouldn't be surprising: after all, brains have been evolving for over half a billion years! (Tae-Yoon et al., 2018.) Evolution is a random process, of small changes occurring at random times, for no particular reason. Evolution has no end goal: some of those random changes stick and some don't – although the useful changes do tend to stick around longer than the harmful ones.

The way our brains learn has evolved alongside the brain itself. The end result? In learning, evolution has concocted for us a complex, messy process. One that isn't easy to understand. One preoccupied with solving problems – like surviving in nature – that we're no longer concerned with solving. Nobody can tell you for certain how learning occurs, but we're finding out more every year.

Here's what we know so far . . .

What does it mean to learn?

If we want to learn about the inner workings of learning, it'd be helpful to start with a crisp definition. Given we learn new things all the time, often without even trying, learning may at first seem like a simple process. Mirriam-Webster defines "learning" as:

learning (noun)

learn·​ing | \ ˈlər-niŋ \

  1. : the activity or process of gaining knowledge or skill by studying, practicing, being taught, or experiencing something : the activity of someone who learns

This definition already gives a hint at underlying complexity: learning is an activity or a process. (Shunck, 2020) expands on this definition, giving us more of an idea of what learning might involve (p. 2):

Learning involves acquiring and modifying knowledge, skills, strategies, beliefs, attitudes, and behaviors. People learn cognitive, behavioral, linguistic, motor, and social skills, and these can take many forms.

Learning consists of a set of ongoing processes that go back and forth, feeding into each other. Here are some of them:

Conceptualization

We think an idea we've never thought before.

Elaboration

We derive new ideas from things we already knew.

Contextualization

We draw new connections between ideas we already knew.

Reinforcement

By practicing a thought process, we make it easier to repeat it in the future.

Generalization

We recognize a common pattern between things that used to seem unrelated.

Reorganization

We change the way we remember things based on new patterns and generalizations.

Transfer

We apply a pattern that we learned in one context, and apply it in a new, unrelated context.

These are just some of the ways the brain processes information as part of learning. There are certainly more – probably even some nobody has thought of yet!

It might be tempting to think of these as a progression: for example, to assume that one first conceptualizes a new idea, then elaborates on it, contextualizes it, identifies generalizations and works toward transfer. Certainly there are some interdependencies between these processes, but remember, the brain is not such an orderly system! These processes go back and forth, feeding into one another from one idea to the next.

It only takes a moment for these processes to take place in the mind. In that way, learning happens in fits and starts.

Clearly, these things can't happen in a vacuum — when we learn, our minds take an active role in processing information, but how did that information get into our minds in the first place? It seems we have more to learn . . .

Sensing & Memory

The set of things we're thinking about right now is called working memory.

We don't know exactly what working memory is from a neurological standpoint, nor do we know everything about how it works. But we do know that we don't have much of it: studies show most people can keep about seven different ideas or pieces of information in working memory at a time (Schunk, 2020). Ever wonder why phone numbers are seven digits long?

7 ± 2

We also know working memory is fragile: as our working memory fills up, ideas that were already in working memory are destroyed by new incoming information. Scientists who study memory call this process interference (Rasch & Born, 2013).

The seven cognitive processes we discussed earlier – conceptualization, elaboration, contextualization, reinforcement, generatlization, reorganization and transfer – all operate on ideas stored in working memory. So, to complete our picture of how learning occurs in the brain, what we really need to know is:

How does information get into working memory?

There are two routes: sensing and recall. Let's start with sensing.

Our senses provide us with rich, detailed information about the world. That information ends up in working memory, where we process it. But there's a problem: working memory is tiny! How can we cut all that rich sensory data down into just a few key pieces of information?

The short answer is we don't have to – at least not consciously! Our minds are constantly scanning our senses, automatically picking things that seem 'important' to move into working memory, and throwing everything else away. In everday language, we call this process "noticing."

As a mental process, noticing derives from our genetic history. We tend to notice things that would help us identify opportunities and threats while fending for ourselves in the natural world. Things like:

  • Loud sounds, sudden sounds, and human voices (especially babies)
  • Bright things, movement and human faces
  • Patterns we've recognized before

These are just some of our built-in mental tools for noticing. Nobody has a complete list!

Other than sensing and noticing, there's another way to get information into working memory: recall. That's a fancy way of saying "remembering stuff."

We've already talked a little about working memory. Recall involves a second type of memory known as long-term memory. Specifically, recall means activating an idea in long-term memory, thereby adding it to working memory. Once the idea has been recalled into working memory, we can process it.

Like working memory, we don't completely understand long-term memory – but we are actively doing research! Right now, many scientists believe memories are formed via a two-step process (Rasch & Born, 2013):

Encoding: Thoughts in working memory are first 'encoded' into long-term memory. This happens while we're awake, and the resulting memories are plastic: easy to change, and easy to forget!

Consolidation: When we sleep, newly encoded memories are consolidated, with each other and with older memories. This process seems to change the way we link memories together, and makes them more stable (less plastic).

Surprisingly, our brains will go ahead and encode new long-term memories from anything in our working memories – even ideas that we just retrieved from long-term memory! In fact, studies confirm repeatedly recalling an idea can distort it: when you remember the recalled idea, it gets mixed with other ideas that were also in working memory at the same time before then being re-encoded as a new long-term memory (Bridge & Paller, 2012).

On the other hand, recalling a memory also seems to reinforce it, thereby making it easier to recall it again in the future. Paradoxically, this implies we need to recall key ideas to learn a new subject area, but at the same time, the act of recalling those ideas may itself distort them. No wonder so much of learning involves assessment and correction!

In Review

What do we know about learning so far?

We know that we get information into working memory via sensing and long-term recall. Once we have information in working memory, we know we can process it – elaborate, contextualize, generalize and so on. This results in new ideas, which get encoded and consolidated into long-term memory. Those ideas are then available for recall when we need them again. Repeat the process on a large enough body of ideas, and eventually we master something bigger!

That might seem like a pretty complete cognitive model of learning. But we're still missing a big piece – a uniquely human one ...

Metacognition

So far, we've been talking about brain processes that occur unconsciously and automatically: sensing, memory, cognitive processing. In lower-order animal brains, these processes are always unconscious and automatic, at least as far as we can tell. But humans can change the equation: we can consciously direct just about every aspect of learning:

Sensing: Although our minds automatically pick things to pay attention to — such as loud noises or movement — humans can also deliberately pay attention to other things. Do you think you would be reading this text on this screen right now, were it not for your deliberate decision to do so?

Recall: Although our minds can automatically recall ideas that relate to a sensory cue, we can also deliberately choose to recall specific ideas. Can you remember a time you did really well on a test or an assignment?

Processing: Although our minds automatically process some information, we can choose to direct what we process and how. For example, try comparing and contrasting cats and dogs right now. Did you draw any generalizations from what you know about cats and dogs?

We call our ability to direct our own thought processes executive control. Executive control encompasses everything listed above, and more.

Scientifically, we know humans have executive control, and we know several things can do using their executive control. However, we don't have a very clear picture of what causes a brain to activate its executive abilities. That said, motivation appears to be a large influence. Let's talk about that next!

Motivation

Like learning, motivation is a complex system involving many cognitive processes working together.

At its core, we believe motivation is a reward/punishment system, driving us to seek rewarding things and avoid punishment. Psychologists call this conditioning. In other words, our motivational systems drive us to repeat rewarding behaviors and avoid punishment.

Just like with learning, our brains come with some automatic motivational behaviors that seem to be based on our genetic ancestors' need to survive in the natural world. These include:

To Do

Survival: Basic living functions such as eating, sleeping and reproduction are rewarding and thus reinforcing. Things which threaten our survival, like hunger, pain and disease are punishing – we tend to avoid them.

Social: We find friendship and feelings status or worth within a group rewarding, and seek out social connections. Ostracization and rejection are punishing, and we avoid behaviors that would kick us out of the group.

Achievement: Creative expression and succeededing at meaningfully difficult tasks are both rewarding; failure is punishing.

Motivators don't have to be self-centered; we can also be motivated by empathy: our mental models of other people's mental states. We find other people's rewarding experiences to be rewarding oursleves, and we find other people's punishments to be punishing ourselves – especially when we had a hand in causing them.

Just like with learning, humans have metacognitive control over motivation:

We can deliberately influence how motivational signals influence us. We can choose to ignore punishing signals (like exercise being unpleasant today) or focus on imagined or yet-unrealized rewards (like being in shape). However, this only works for a while: eventually, once we have expended our 'will power,' we're left with limited control over motivation.

We also have indirect control over our motivation. For example, we can set goals and monitor our progress toward those goals, thereby constructing our own rewards and punishments.

Certain key beliefs about ourselves appear to influence how motivational signals affect our behavior. (Dweck, 2007) described a growth mindset as a set of self-beliefs centered around one's ability to learn and change, as opposed to a fixed mindset which stresses innate capacities that do not change. Dweck found that people with growth mindsets had stronger reactions to rewards (realized and unrealized) and weaker reactions to punishment, helping drive a pattern of persistence that leads to success.

Essential Questions

What do you need to know about learning to be an effective teacher?

How does a learner move from an unknowing state to a knowing state?

At a basic level, the learner must first conceptualize the main idea. Some basic facts and concepts can be directly observed through the senses. Other, more abstract facts can be communicated, orally or written. The most abstract concepts are very difficult to communicate at all – sometimes, engaging in a thought activity like problem solving is the most effective way to reach an idea.

Most people would argue that 'knowing' involves remembering, at least for a while. Learners best remember what they know when they repeat a thought process, thereby reinforcing it. However, recall distorts memories. Learners remember correctly when recall is paired with assessment and immediate corrective feedback.

Many advanced concepts and skills pull are built on bodies of knowledge, rather standing alone. To develop these, learners build a network of interrelated knowledge.

Which internal factors affect learning?

Learners differ in their ability to carry out cognitive skills like logic, critical thinking, problem solving and pattern recognition. Although these skills can be developed, the learner's current ability determines which the learner can use to work with a new idea.

A learner's existing body of knowledge, skills and experiences determines which ideas a learner is "ready" to process next. Vygotsky called this set of ideas the "Zone of Proximal Development," or "ZPD" (Schunk, 2020). New ideas outside a learner's ZPD draw on knowledge or skills the learner doesn't yet have, making it infeasible to learn those ideas right now.

A learner's individual working memory capacity affects their ability to process information and come up with new ideas. A learner's working memory capacity is not fixed: for example, if the learner's relationship isn't going well, or is going really well, the relationship may reside in the learner's working memory – taking away capacity that could have been used for learning.

Several factors, such as the learner's interest in the content, the learner's feelings about how related the content is to their own life, the learner's mindset and current energy levels affect their motivation for learning.

Which external factors affect learning?

One major external factor is how well the content is delivered – that is, how easily the learner is able to conceptualize the right internal ideas for the content. Factors of content delivery include how the content is broken down into chunks, how clearly and concisely ideas are communicated, and the way the content is presented (which may be written, oral, visual and so on). The mode of communication is especially relevant for students with visual impairments, hearing impairments or dyslexia.

The context in which content is delivered also impacts learning. Learning is more effective in an environment free from distractions like noise or movement, and safe enough that learners do not need to use their mental resources to worry about their own physical or emotional safety.

How does memory impact learning?

There are two kinds of memory: working memory and long-term memory.

Working memory has several immediate impacts on learning and cognition. One ramification of working memory is its limited capacity: the human mind has many heuristics that determine what will or will not be noticed, and learning is more effective when all key ideas make it past these filters. In human terms, this means learning is most effective when the learners are engaged, focused, or paying attention.

Working memory is also involved in key cognitive processes like elaboration, contextualization, generalization and transfer. Learning is more effective when learners are thinking about the right things, and aren't distracted.

Long-term memory is involved in learners' ability to recall an idea or repeat a task long after it was initially learned. Learning is more effective when key ideas are repeated, with immediate corrective feedback, as well as when key ideas are presented in a variety of different related contexts.

What role does motivation play in learning?

Although learning is automatic in low-order animal brains, humans play an active role in learning. We call this ability to regulate our own learning processes executive control. In turn, how and when we exercise our executive control is affected by motivation – an entirely separate system on mental functions. An unmotivated learner is thus unlikely to play an active role in his or her own learning, and thereby learn ineffectively, if at all!

How does transfer occur, and why does it matter?

Transfer occurs when a learner realizes that a pattern gleaned from one context applies to a new learning context.

Transfer can be considered a sign of mastery: to successfully transfer knowledge to a new context, a learner would first need to have conceptualized the idea, elaborated on it, understood its original context, reinforced the idea until it was easy to recall, and identified a generalized pattern from the idea, in order to finally transfer that pattern to a new context. Prompting a learner to try to transfer patterns between situations is thus a way to help guide the learner through all the key learning processes we discussed earlier.

Finally, transfer is often at the heart of coming up with a clever insight, making transfer not only the pinnacle of learning, but one of the first key steps to advancing human knowledge.
